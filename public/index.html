Our History

Inspired by Walden

Like Thoreau's retreat to Walden Pond, our community embraces the idea that the richest life is one lived close to nature.

Walden's Revisited is a small, covenant-protected neighborhood located in Scioto Township, Delaware County, Ohio. Our community is built around a central pond and shared common areas that connect neighbors to the land and to each other.

Situated along Brindle Road (Township Road 170) near Slocum Road, our tracts of land offer the space, quiet, and natural beauty that define rural Ohio living—while being just a short drive from the amenities of Delaware and Columbus.

Thoreau was more than a naturalist—he was a philosopher of self-governance. In Civil Disobedience, he argued that individual conscience must guide action above all else, because government, however well-intentioned, can be weaponized as an instrument of oppression against the very people it claims to serve. He believed that small, deliberate communities built on mutual respect and shared principle are stronger than any authority imposed from above. That spirit lives here. Walden's Revisited is not governed by force or bureaucracy, but by the voluntary commitments we made to each other—recorded in ink, bound by law, and rooted in the belief that neighbors acting in good faith can govern themselves.

“I went to the woods because I wished to live deliberately, to front only the essential facts of life.”

— Henry David Thoreau, Walden

A defined common use easement runs through portions of several tracts within Walden's Revisited. This easement—surveyed by Scioto Land Surveying Service and recorded with Delaware County (Book 1559, Pages 2106–2108)—encompasses the central pond and surrounding natural areas while individual homesites remain private.

The easement boundaries are precisely defined by metes and bounds survey, recorded with Delaware County (Book 1559, Pages 2106–2108). The survey image shown here is taken from the official filing of the metes and bounds of the common areas.

The common areas are maintained collectively by the property owners and are governed by the community's protective covenants, restrictions, and Ohio state law.

Governing Documents

Covenants, Restrictions & Easements

Walden's Revisited is governed by the Restated Declaration of Protective Covenants and Restrictions, originally filed August 24, 2015 (Book 1372, Pages 1033–1064) and restated in 2019 with Delaware County. These are binding legal documents that apply to every property owner in the community.

Common Use Easements

The common use easements within Walden's Revisited are permanent appurtenant easements that run with the land. This means:

  • They transfer automatically with the property. When a lot is sold, the easement rights and obligations pass to the new owner. No separate agreement is needed—the easements are part of the deed.
  • They are independent of the HOA. The easements exist as recorded interests in the land. Even if the homeowners association were to change in structure, the easements themselves would remain in full force and effect.
  • They cannot be unilaterally removed. Under Ohio law, appurtenant easements that benefit and burden multiple properties cannot be terminated by any single owner or even a majority vote. Termination of the easements would require the unanimous consent of all property owners.
  • They are recorded with Delaware County. The easement boundaries are defined by a professional metes and bounds survey (Book 1559, Pages 2106–2108) and are a matter of public record.

Planned Community Under Ohio Law

Because Walden's Revisited has common elements (the easements), it is classified as a “planned community” under the Ohio Planned Community Act (Ohio Revised Code Chapter 5312). This has several important implications:

  • The community must maintain an owners association as long as common elements exist.
  • Under ORC §5312.05(B), a vote to terminate the declaration and dissolve the planned community requires the unanimous consent of all owners—not a simple majority.
  • The association has legal authority to assess dues, maintain common areas, and enforce the declaration.
  • Delinquent assessments may result in a lien filed against the lot under ORC Chapter 5312, which can be foreclosed upon.

Community Lifestyle Protections

Many homeowners don't realize the scope of protections the 2019 Restated Declaration provides. These aren't abstract legal provisions—they're the rules that directly affect your daily quality of life, your property value, and the character of the neighborhood you chose to live in.

Dangerous & Nuisance Animals

No animals may be kept that create a danger or nuisance to other residents. Livestock, poultry, and other non-domestic animals are prohibited. Dogs and cats are permitted, but owners are responsible for ensuring their pets do not endanger or disturb neighbors. (Section 3.15)

Noise & Nuisance Prohibition

No noxious, illegal, or offensive activity is permitted on any lot, and nothing may be done that becomes an annoyance or nuisance to the neighborhood. This protects every homeowner from unreasonable disturbances. (Section 3.01)

Firearm Discharge Prohibited

The discharge of firearms within the subdivision is strictly prohibited. This includes all types of guns and applies to all lots and common areas, ensuring safety for residents, children, and guests throughout the community. (Section 3.17)

No Hunting

Hunting of any kind is prohibited within the boundaries of Walden's Revisited. Despite the rural setting, the community prioritized the safety of all residents and the peaceful enjoyment of the natural environment over recreational hunting. (Section 3.17)

Short-Term Rental Ban (Airbnb)

No lot may be used for transient or short-term leasing, including hotel-style or Airbnb-type rentals. This prevents a revolving door of strangers in the neighborhood and preserves the residential community character that property values depend on. (Section 3.08)

Vehicle & Parking Restrictions

Commercial vehicles, boats, trailers, RVs, and similar equipment may not be permanently parked on streets or in visible areas of lots. Inoperable or unregistered vehicles are prohibited. These rules prevent the neighborhood from looking like a storage lot. (Section 3.11)

Fence Standards

All fences must be approved by the Board and meet specific material and height requirements. This prevents unsightly barriers that can diminish neighboring property values and ensures a consistent, attractive streetscape throughout the community. (Section 3.12)

Single-Family Residential Only

Every lot is restricted to single-family residential use. No commercial businesses, multi-family dwellings, or industrial activities may operate from any property. This is the foundational protection that keeps the neighborhood a neighborhood. (Section 3.01)

Signage Restrictions

Signage is limited to standard real estate signs during active sales. Political signs, commercial advertising, and other signage that detracts from the residential character of the community are restricted. (Section 3.13)

Refuse & Property Maintenance

All trash and recyclable containers must be stored out of public view except on collection days. Lots must be maintained in a clean and orderly condition. No accumulation of junk, debris, or unsightly materials is permitted. (Section 3.14)

Exterior Modifications Require Approval

Any exterior modifications, additions, or construction must be submitted to and approved by the Board before work begins. This ensures that changes to individual properties don't negatively impact the aesthetic standards or value of neighboring homes. (Section 3.04)

No Subdivision of Lots

Lots may not be further subdivided below the minimum size requirements. This prevents the breakup of properties into smaller parcels that would change the density and rural character that defines Walden's Revisited. (Section 3.03)

None of these protections existed in the 2015 original. Under the original declaration, there were virtually no lifestyle restrictions beyond basic building requirements. A neighbor could have operated an Airbnb, discharged firearms, let aggressive animals roam, or parked commercial vehicles indefinitely—and there would have been no legal mechanism to stop them. The 2019 Restatement changed that.

Declaration Overview

Owners Association

All lot owners are automatically members of the Walden's Revisited Homeowners Association. The association is governed by a Board of Directors and operates under recorded bylaws. Each lot is entitled to one vote on association matters.

Assessments & Dues

Annual assessments are established in the declaration to fund maintenance of common areas and association operations. All lot owners are obligated to pay assessments. Unpaid dues accrue interest and may become a lien on the property.

Common Area Maintenance

The association is responsible for maintaining the common use easement areas, including the pond and surrounding land. All owners share in this responsibility through their assessments.

Land Use & Building

The declaration establishes guidelines for residential use, building standards, and activities on the lots to preserve the rural character and natural beauty of the community.

Amendments

The declaration may be amended by a two-thirds vote of the lot owners (per Section 1.10 of the original declaration, when six or more lots have occupied homes). Termination of the declaration or easements requires unanimous consent.

Enforcement

The association and individual lot owners have standing to enforce the covenants and restrictions. Violations may be addressed through the Board, and legal remedies are available under the declaration and Ohio law.

The Permanent Reality: Common Area Access Under Both Declarations

The most critical fact for every homeowner to understand is that both the 2015 and 2019 Declarations permanently guarantee access to the common areas for all lot owners and their guests. The 2019 Restatement did not create this access—it was permanently established in 2015 and carried forward.

  • The original 2015 document explicitly states that “Permanent easements across various Parcels will run with the land and are for the common use of all residents and their invited guests.” Every resident has a “right and easement for the enjoyment and use of the Common Properties.” These easements are “perpetually in force and effect.”
  • There is no mechanism to easily dissolve these common areas. Because the easements are appurtenant to the land (meaning they run with the deed), removing them requires unanimous consent from every single deed holder.
  • Mortgage lenders must also approve removal. When a bank issues a mortgage, the loan is secured by the property as legally described in the deed. Because access to common areas (like the pond) contributes to the appraised value of the collateral, any attempt to strip those easements requires formal, written consent from every bank holding a mortgage in the neighborhood. This makes removing the common areas virtually impossible.

Where Ohio State Law (ORC 5312) Overrides Both Documents

Walden's Revisited meets the legal definition of a “Planned Community” under the Ohio Planned Community Act (ORC Chapter 5312). Because the 2015 document mandated that owners join a “Community Association,” required the payment of annual assessments, and dictated that these funds maintain common properties, it firmly placed the neighborhood under state jurisdiction.

Where the community's documents are silent or vague, Ohio law automatically fills the gap:

Bylaws & Governance

State Mandate: All HOAs must have legally recorded bylaws to operate.

Liability Insurance

State Mandate: Requires the HOA to carry Commercial General Liability insurance for common areas, as well as Directors & Officers (D&O) coverage.

Financial Reserves

State Mandate: Requires the HOA to adopt an annual budget with fully funded reserves for major capital repairs (unless owners vote annually to waive it).

Property Restrictions

No Override: State law leaves aesthetic and lifestyle rules strictly up to the recorded Declarations.

Why the 2019 Restated Declaration Is Superior

The 2015 document placed the neighborhood under strict state mandates—like requiring maintenance of common areas and exposing the community to liability—but provided absolutely no legal mechanism to manage them. The 2019 Restatement provides the legal “machinery” required to comply with Ohio law and actively protects homeowners.

A

The “Shield” — Collective Liability Protection

2015 Flaw: Under the 2015 rules, homeowners share a permanent easement but have no corporate HOA insurance mandate. If someone is injured in the common area (e.g., the pond), personal injury lawyers can potentially sue the homeowners collectively as the holders of the easement.

2019 Improvement: The 2019 document legally mandates the HOA to purchase a liability policy. This acts as a corporate shield, protecting individual homeowners' personal assets from lawsuits arising from common-area accidents.

Why This Matters More Than Most People Realize

One of the most important but often overlooked benefits of having our HOA in effect is the liability protection it provides to every homeowner in the subdivision. When the Association authorizes maintenance on Common Areas—tree removal, pond management, dock repairs, pavilion upkeep—it does so as a legal entity, through Board resolutions, with licensed and insured contractors. Any claim arising from that work is directed at the Association and its insurance carrier, not at you personally.

Without that structure, any individual who took it upon themselves to maintain Common Areas—even with the best of intentions—would carry the full weight of that risk alone. If something went wrong during the work (property damage, personal injury), they would have no contractor insurance to fall back on.

Worse still, because every lot owner holds an interest in the Common Areas, that same well-meaning person could face claims from fellow neighbors for performing unauthorized alterations to commonly owned property, regardless of how necessary the work seemed. Just as someone who performs maintenance can seek reimbursement from all homeowners, the inverse is equally true: any person who performs unauthorized maintenance or changes to the Common Areas could face legal action from all the individuals in the neighborhood.

The HOA eliminates that impossible position. It gives us a proper mechanism to identify what needs to be done, authorize it through legitimate governance, and ensure that the person who cares enough to raise the issue isn't the one left holding the bag. It protects the people who want to do the right thing, and it protects the rest of us from the consequences of uncoordinated, uninsured work happening on property we all share.

B

The “Engine” — Operational Machinery

2015 Flaw: The 2015 document says assessment funds can be used to “maintain the landscape” but gives no instructions on how to elect a board, establish a quorum, sign contracts, or legally authorize those expenses.

2019 Improvement: It provides a comprehensive, legally compliant 16-page “Code of Regulations” (Bylaws). This gives the community the actual legal framework required by the state to open a bank account, elect a board of directors, buy the required insurance, and hire vendors to fulfill maintenance mandates.

C

The “Walls” — Neighborhood Lifestyle Protections

2015 Flaw: The original document is essentially the “Wild West.” As long as you build a single-family home of a certain size with an attached garage, there are almost no lifestyle restrictions.

2019 Improvement: The 2019 Restatement introduces standard protections that maintain modern property values. It strictly regulates fence materials and heights, bans the permanent street parking of commercial vehicles and boats, and prohibits transient or short-term leasing (like hotel-style Airbnbs).

What Every Homeowner Should Know

1

Your easement rights are permanent — under both declarations

The common use easements were established in the original 2015 Declaration and are appurtenant to the land. They do not expire, they transfer automatically with every sale, and they are carried forward in the 2019 Restatement. Neither document allows them to be removed without unanimous consent of all owners and their mortgage lenders.

2

Assessments are a legal obligation

Every lot owner is required to pay annual assessments as established in the declaration. These funds maintain the common areas that benefit all property owners. Failure to pay may result in liens and legal action under Ohio law.

3

The HOA exists because the easements exist

Under Ohio's Planned Community Act, as long as common elements (easements) exist, the community must have an owners association. The association and the easements are interconnected by law.

4

Changes require unanimous consent

Under ORC §5312.05(B), terminating the declaration or dissolving the planned community requires the consent of every property owner—100%, not a simple majority. This protects all owners' rights equally.

5

The 2019 Restatement protects you — the 2015 original did not

The 2015 Declaration created the obligations (easements, assessments, shared liability) but provided no operational framework to manage them. The 2019 Restatement provides the liability insurance mandate, elected board governance, recorded bylaws, and lifestyle protections that Ohio law requires and modern communities need.
