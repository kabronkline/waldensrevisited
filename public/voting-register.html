What Every Signer Explicitly Agreed To

The 2019 Restated Declaration is not a vague petition or a statement of interest. It is a 52-page legally binding instrument recorded with the Delaware County Recorder's Office. Every owner who signed the document on March 9, 2019 affixed their signature to the full text of this instrument—including all of the following provisions:

  • Creation of an HOA Corporation Signers approved the formation of the Walden's Revisited Homeowners Association as a formal Ohio nonprofit corporation with full legal authority to govern the community. (Article II)
  • Mandatory Membership Signers agreed that every lot owner is automatically and irrevocably a member of the association. Membership is not optional and cannot be declined. (Section 2.02)
  • Payment of Annual Dues & Assessments Signers agreed to pay annual assessments as determined by the Board of Directors, with the understanding that unpaid dues accrue interest, become a lien on the property, and are enforceable through foreclosure under Ohio law. (Article IV)
  • Adherence to All Bylaws Signers approved the attached 16-page Code of Regulations (Bylaws), agreeing to be bound by all rules governing board elections, meetings, quorum requirements, and decision-making procedures. (Exhibit B)
  • Liability Insurance Mandate Signers agreed that the HOA must carry Commercial General Liability insurance and Directors & Officers coverage, funded through their assessments. (Section 6.01)
  • Enforcement & Legal Remedies Signers consented to the association's authority to enforce all covenants, restrictions, and rules through legal action, including injunctive relief and recovery of attorney's fees. (Article VII)
A signature on this document is not a suggestion—it is a binding legal commitment. Every signer acknowledged the full scope of these obligations. The recorded instrument with each owner's original signature is a matter of permanent public record at the Delaware County Recorder's Office.

How This Vote Was Validated

The original Declaration (Section 1.10) provides that it may be amended by a two-thirds vote when six or more lots have homes constructed upon them which are occupied. Under Ohio law (ORC 5312.01(J)), a “lot” is defined as a tract with a separate parcel number assigned by the county auditor. Following two property consolidations, the Delaware County Auditor recognizes 25 legal lots in the community. Amending the declaration therefore requires 75% approval—a minimum of 19 signatures. With 20 of 25 lots signing, the Restated Declaration cleared this threshold and is the currently effective governing document.

Under the Declaration, there is one vote per lot, regardless of how many individuals are listed as owners on a given parcel. In the absence of a dispute by co-owners, a lot's vote can be made by any one co-owner.

How the Lot Count Reached 25: A Legal History

The voting register contains 25 rows rather than the 26 referenced in the original declaration text. Understanding why is critical to understanding the community's governance. The table below cross-references each lot and owner for those who wish to verify the specifics.

1. The Original Drafting Discrepancy (2014–2015)

The confusion begins with a fundamental contradiction in the original 2015 Declaration. The text of the document explicitly claimed Walden's Revisited consisted of 26 lots. However, mapping out the properties designated in the original recorded exhibits (Lots 1–4, Tracts 1–15, and Tracts 20–27) yields exactly 27 distinct platted tracts. This indicates a clear counting or drafting error by the original declarant from the very beginning.

2. The County Consolidations (2016–2017)

Following the initial development, two separate property mergers occurred. The owners of Lot 2 and Lot 3 (parcel 20040001060004) consolidated their properties into a single 5-acre parcel. Similarly, the owners of Tract 10 and Tract 11 (parcel 20040001061010) combined theirs. Because the Delaware County Auditor officially recognized these joined properties as single tax parcels, the physical number of distinct tracts in the community dropped from the platted 27 down to 25.

3. The Mathematical and Legal Impact on Voting

This consolidation is the linchpin for the community's governance because of how the state calculates voting power.

  • The Legal Definition: Under the Ohio Planned Community Law (ORC 5312.01(J)), a “lot” is explicitly defined as a tract of land that has a “separate parcel number assigned by the county auditor.”
  • The Denominator Shift: Because the county auditor officially reduced the number of parcel numbers, the legal denominator for the HOA permanently dropped to 25 lots, rendering the 26-lot text in the 2015 Declaration legally obsolete.
  • The Voting Threshold: Amending the declaration requires 75% approval. 75% of 25 lots is 18.75. Since fractional votes do not exist, a minimum of 19 signatures is required to pass any amendment.
  • The Outcome: The 20 signatures on the 2019 Restated Declaration successfully cleared this 19-vote threshold. Had state law not deferred to the county auditor's parcel count (keeping the denominator at the platted 27), 21 signatures would have been required, and the 2019 restatement would have failed.
  • The Bottom Line: Even if the original declaration's stated total of 26 lots were used as the denominator instead of the county auditor's 25, the outcome does not change. 75% of 26 is 19.5—requiring 20 signatures. The 2019 Restated Declaration received exactly 20 signatures, clearing the 75% threshold under any reasonable interpretation of the lot count. Whether the number is 25, 26, or even the original platted 27, the vote passed because two owners owning two lots had only one vote. The 75% threshold is cleared in all cases therefore the restated declaration is in effect.

4. Impact on Reinstatement, Assessments, and Easements

Validating the 25-lot count has profound implications for the legal landscape of the community moving forward:

  • HOA Reinstatement: Because the 2019 vote mathematically succeeded under Ohio law, the Restated Declaration is the undisputed, legally binding governing document. Any formal reinstatement of the dissolved HOA, election of officers, or enforcement of bylaws must strictly adhere to the 2019 framework.
  • Financial Assessments: When calculating budgets, annual dues, or potential special assessments for the reinstated association, all costs must be divided 25 ways. The consolidated properties (Lot 2/3 and Tract 10/11) legally possess only one vote and carry only one share of the financial liability.
  • Common Area Easements: The legal survival of the 2019 Restatement permanently cements the rules surrounding the permanent common areas. While facing situations that act as a deterrent to enjoying these shared spaces can be highly frustrating, the legal right to maintain ongoing access and continue utilizing the common areas remains fully protected and enforceable under these validated covenants.

Notes on Parcel Consolidations

The original subdivision contained 27 platted lots/tracts. Two properties have since been legally consolidated by their respective owners, reducing the total to 25 current tax parcels:

1. Lot 2/3: Originally platted as Lot 2 and Lot 3. Combined into a single 5.001-acre property. The Delaware County Auditor now recognizes this as a single parcel (20040001060004).

2. Tract 10/11: Originally platted as Tract 10 and Tract 11. Combined into a single property under parcel 20040001061010.

Important: Parcel consolidation does not affect the covenants. The consolidated parcels remain subject to all easements, restrictions, and obligations under the Restated Declaration. Each consolidated parcel carries one vote in association matters.
